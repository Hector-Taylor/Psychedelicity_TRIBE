# Refined framing: contextual computation as motivation, not the whole proposal

## The adjustment

You are right.

The proposal should **not** become “the Aqil project.” That narrows it too much and makes the whole thing feel derivative. The center of gravity should remain:

- a **methodological contribution**,
- a **computational perturbation framework** for naturalistic stimuli,
- and a set of **intended findings** about which sensory dimensions most strongly and systematically alter predicted cortical response structure.

The contextual-computation literature should enter as a **motivating bridge**, not as the entire conceptual superstructure.

That means the opening logic should be:

1. Psychedelic visual phenomenology suggests that perception can be altered along identifiable sensory dimensions.
2. Recent work in visual psychophysics and neuroimaging indicates that at least some of these alterations may involve changes in **contextual visual computations**.
3. This motivates a computational search over naturalistic stimulus perturbations designed to identify which dimensions most strongly shift predicted cortical responses.
4. The resulting perturbation map can then guide future human studies.

That is cleaner. It keeps the proposal yours.

## How Aqil should appear

Aqil’s work should do **three jobs**, and only three jobs.

### 1. Justify the sensory level as scientifically serious
You are not studying “mere visuals.” Sensory-level alterations are potentially mechanistically relevant.

### 2. Motivate contextual dimensions as one especially promising family
Figure-ground structure, local context, surround interactions, and temporal persistence are worth perturbing because recent evidence suggests they matter.

### 3. Strengthen the interpretation of downstream findings
If some perturbation families disproportionately affect predicted cortical responses, the contextual-computation literature gives you a principled language for interpreting that pattern.

That is enough. Do not let it swallow the project.

## Better hypothesis structure

### Primary hypothesis
Controlled perturbations of distinct sensory dimensions in naturalistic videos will produce dissociable and quantifiable changes in TRIBE-predicted cortical response geometry.

### Secondary hypothesis
A low-dimensional perturbation manifold will emerge from these model-predicted response changes, revealing a compact set of computationally meaningful axes along which sensory alterations can be organized.

### Motivated exploratory hypothesis
Perturbations affecting contextual structure—such as figure-ground stability, local surround relationships, and temporal persistence—may exert especially strong or distinctive effects, consistent with recent work suggesting that psychedelic states can alter contextual visual computations.

That is the right balance.
Aqil enters in hypothesis 3, not in hypothesis 1.

## Better “significance” framing

The significance is not:
“we test Aqil in silico.”

The significance is:

1. We provide a **general method** for constructing and analyzing parametric sensory perturbation spaces in naturalistic stimuli.
2. We use a state-of-the-art brain encoding model to rank which perturbations most strongly alter predicted cortical responses.
3. We generate a principled, preregisterable basis for selecting stimuli for future human neuroimaging work.
4. We connect the resulting perturbation axes to candidate mechanisms from perception research, including contextual visual computation.

That is elegant and proportional.

## Better workshop blurb
### ~300 words

We propose a preregistered open-data project using BOLD Moments and TRIBE v2 to develop a computational framework for parameterizing sensory perturbations in naturalistic audiovisual stimuli. Our primary hypothesis is that controlled perturbations of distinct sensory dimensions will produce dissociable changes in predicted cortical response geometry, allowing the construction of a low-dimensional perturbation manifold with interpretable computational structure. Using short naturalistic video clips from BOLD Moments, we will generate graded perturbations spanning dimensions such as contour stability, figure-ground structure, temporal persistence, local contextual interference, and color dynamics, and evaluate their effects on TRIBE-predicted responses. We will quantify whole-cortex and region-level changes, compare perturbation families, and test whether a compact set of latent axes captures the dominant structure of these effects. The project is motivated in part by recent findings that psychedelic states may alter contextual visual computations, suggesting that perturbations of local context and segmentation may be especially informative, ~~but its main contribution is methodological~~: an open, preregistered approach for screening candidate stimulus manipulations before follow-up human studies. Expected outputs include a ranked perturbation atlas, a reproducible analysis pipeline, and a principled basis for designing future neuroimaging experiments.

We propose a collaborative, preregistered project using BOLD Moments and TRIBE v2 to develop a computational framework for sensory perturbations in naturalistic audiovisual stimuli. Our hypothesis is that psychedelic visual phenomenology reflects structured shifts in sensory processing, including contextual visual computations, and that controlled perturbations of distinct sensory dimensions will therefore produce dissociable changes in predicted cortical response geometry. In particular, perturbations affecting figure-ground structure, local context, and temporal persistence may be especially informative. Using the open BOLD Moments fMRI dataset of short naturalistic video clips, we will generate graded perturbations of original stimuli, run both original and perturbed clips through TRIBE v2, and quantify changes in predicted cortical responses using whole-cortex distances, ROI-level analyses, and dimensionality-reduction methods to identify a low-dimensional perturbation manifold. The significance of the project is methodological and translational: it provides a preregisterable framework for linking psychedelic phenomenology to computationally interpretable stimulus dimensions, and it offers a principled way to select candidate stimuli for future human neuroimaging studies. Estimated cost for the workshop phase is minimal, consisting primarily of compute and researcher time, with no new data collection required.
